The oddest use of the Pyramid might have been in “Four Extraordinary Skyscrapers,” a 2005 publication by Running Press. Transamerica was chopped into cards that, when assembled, formed one side of a 2-foot-high illuminated screen around a table lamp. Photo: Running Press

The local revue “Beach Blanket Babylon” has never been shy about milking the Transamerica Pyramid for laughs. Here is one of the many supersize hats worn by cast members where the pointy tower tops things off. Photo: Rick Markovich

When dressmaker Alvin Duskin got an initiative on the ballot in 1971 to place a six-story height limit on all new buildings, the alternative weekly Bay Guardian published an entire book to tout the cause — with the Pyramid on the cover, natch.

The Transamerica Pyramid, unveiled Jan. 27, 1969, was originally envisioned as an extremely narrow 1,000-square-foot tower with no pointy ears. The site eventually grew, and the building took the slightly shorter, squatter form we know today.

From certain angles — or if the 40-foot height limits in North Beach and Telegraph Hill are done away with once and for all — the Transamerica Pyramid will stand alone forevermore. Katy Raddatz, The Chronicle

When activists with Occupy SF sought to rally a crowd for a march to defy big banks in the Financial District, the Pyramid was the obvious logo even though Transamerica is an insurance company, with no local employees.

The original design for what now is known as the Transamerica Pyramid, made public in January of 1969, was 1,000 feet tall. That’s even higher than the 853-foot tower that eventually was built — but not quite as tall as what Salesforce Tower someday will be. Courtesy, Aegon USA
